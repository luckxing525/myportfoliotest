§ 01    Lede About

The short
version.

I was born in Beijing and spent my first six years there sketching rocket launches on a small chalkboard — liftoff to orbit, in white chalk, repeatedly, because my parents kept letting me. It was my first attempt at making something complicated feel legible.

Twelve years and three cities later, I’m at NYU Stern studying Business, Technology, and Entrepreneurship. I came in thinking I wanted to be a scientist; the contest-math years in Toronto taught me I love thinking analytically more than I love being faster than other people at it.

The most important thing I’ve made so far is ChatPals — a small startup that connected over a hundred students in rural mountainous China to high-school language tutors in the US and Canada. I started it after thinking about my mother, who grew up in rural China, finished at the top of her class, and was told she could not continue her education because she was a girl. ChatPals didn’t fix that. But it taught me that the things I care about most are stories — whose get told, whose don’t, and how access to one good teacher quietly changes the trajectory of a life.

Right now I’m trying to figure out what I can devote myself to fully. I think it lives somewhere in the overlap of strategy, technology, and the stories that make either one worth doing. 酸甜苦辣 — sour, sweet, bitter, spicy — that’s the whole point.

02 Code · Interactive

CYBERRANGE OS — a phishing
simulator that bites back.

A self-contained, browser-based desktop OS that drops you inside a simulated workstation, hands you an inbox, and waits to see whether you click the suspicious attachment. If you do, the ransomware payload starts to take over the screen — staged, theatrical, and entirely safe. Built in a single HTML file with no frameworks. Open it on any laptop.

04 Hardware · Coursework

A buzzer that listens
to a joystick.

For BTE Lab 04 I built an Arduino instrument: an analog joystick mapped to the pitch of a passive piezo, with the joystick’s click as a mute. The interesting part was the bookkeeping — the analog inputs floated by ±20 at rest, so I had to write a dead-zone and a hysteresis threshold to keep the tone from glitching. Hardware never behaves the way you expect; that was the lesson.
